French citizens living abroad form a small but distinct electorate. They elect 11 of the 577 seats in the National Assembly — about 1.9% — and in the first round of the 2022 presidential election nearly 500,000 of them voted, out of roughly 1.44 million registered (about 1.4% of the national vote).

Every six years they also choose the *conseillers des Français de l'étranger* and *délégués consulaires*: the local representatives who sit on consular councils and, together, form part of the electoral college for the senators representing citizens abroad. The May 2026 consular elections are the freshest read on this electorate's political balance ahead of the 2027 national contests — though, as the data make clear, on a very thin turnout.

# What the early results suggest

These figures are partial — at the time of writing, roughly a third of the constituencies have reported — so they are best read as a first sketch rather than a verdict.

The headline pattern is a tight, fragmented field. Once vote shares are **weighted by the number of valid votes cast** in each constituency (rather than treating a 60-voter post the same as an 18,000-voter one), four blocs cluster within a few points of one another: the Greens and allied lists, the broader left (PS, LFI, *Français du Monde* and others), the centre (Renaissance, MoDem, Horizons and partners), and unaffiliated or "divers" lists. Below them sit the centre-right, the ASFE network, and the far right.

Weighting matters a great deal here. On a simple per-constituency average, unaffiliated and ASFE lists look dominant — but that is largely an artefact of small, low-turnout posts where they do well. Weighting by votes pulls the centre up sharply (its strength is concentrated in large constituencies such as London, Belgium and French-speaking Switzerland) and brings independents back toward the middle of the pack.

The other consistent story is **turnout**, which is very low and uneven: from barely over 1% in New Zealand to a little above 23% in Japan and Scotland. The live figure in the interactive recomputes as results come in.

# Method and choices

A few decisions shape what you see, and reasonable people could make them differently:

- **Party families.** Each list is assigned to one of seven political families based on the party leading it. Many lists are coalitions, so some of these calls are judgment calls — grouping, say, an *FdM*-led list with the left, or *Volt* with the centre. The map colours a constituency by the **leading list**, which is not always the family that won the most seats there (Belgium and Thailand–Burma are good examples of the gap between the two).
- **Vote shares** aggregate each family's first-round scores. In the trend charts they are **vote-weighted** by valid votes cast; constituencies that have not published a vote total are excluded from those averages.
- **Positions** are placed on the relevant consulate city and are approximate; multi-country constituencies are shown at a single indicative point.
- The data mirror the French Wikipedia results table and are updated as consulates report.

# Sources and notes

Results are drawn from the official proclamations published by France's consulates and embassies (via the Ministère de l'Europe et des Affaires étrangères), as compiled on the [French Wikipedia article](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89lections_consulaires_fran%C3%A7aises_de_2026) for the 2026 consular elections.
